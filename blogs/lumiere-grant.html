Winning a 1000 Research Grant at the Lumiere Essay Competition

PM
Prasiddha Mainali AI & ML · St. Xavier's College
Published May 21, 2026
Read Time 6 min read

Introduction

When I got the news, my first reaction wasn't excitement. It was silence. Months of writing, rewriting, deleting entire sections, and genuinely questioning whether my ideas were worth anything — and somehow it led to something real.

💡

The best moments don't always feel like moments when they're happening.

Excellence Award International Recognition
100+ Countries
2000+ Participants

The Thinking

The competition changed how I approach writing. Not as something to sound impressive, but as a way to actually think. Research writing is brutal when you're honest with yourself. Every claim needs backing. Every argument needs to hold up. I rewrote whole sections midway because I realized I was using big words to hide weak understanding. That was uncomfortable. It was also the most useful part.

The Takeaway

The biggest thing I took from this: good writing is just good thinking on paper. The clearest essays usually win, not the most complex ones. Simplicity is harder because it exposes whether you actually understand something. Depth isn't about difficult vocabulary — it's about making a complicated idea easy to follow without losing what makes it complicated.

Clarity is not dumbing it down. It's the hardest form of intelligence.

The Impact

The $1000 grant matters, but not just as money. For students, research is often limited by access — to mentorship, tools, time. Financial support removes some of that barrier. And beyond the practical side, recognition does something quieter: it tells you that the direction you're moving in is worth continuing. Not that you've arrived. Just that it's worth it to keep going.

✅

Pro tip: Before submitting any essay, read it aloud. If you stumble on a sentence, rewrite it. Your ear catches what your eye misses.

Conclusion

Titles and competitions fade. What stays is the skill you built chasing them. This one reinforced something I keep coming back to — consistency outlasts talent almost every time. Most people stop when things get repetitive or uncertain.

The ones who stand out usually just stayed longer.
